CheapoDC Device Build

Six Output CheapoDC Build

The image above shows the front and back sides of a demo/test version of the CheapoDC with six outputs configured. A few points to consider for this build:

  • This 6 output device was built for testing and demonstration purposes, not to be used on an actual astrophotography rig and as such affected some aspects of the design.
  • Because of the first point, there is an LED for each output to help with testing. In general, having a lot of flashing red LEDs on an astrophotography rig is not a good idea. Only the status LED should be needed on a 'production' CheapoDC.
  • This design supports the maximum six outputs supported by CheapoDC firmware. Four outputs have RCA sockets, commonly used for dew straps, and two outputs have 5.5mm X 2.1mm 12 volt sockets. This can be built and The number of outputs and balance of RCA versus 5.5X2.1 sockets can be configured based on personal need. For my personal CheapoDC devices, I use devices designed with two RCA outputs. I only have to control two dew straps on my rigs. The basics of these builds are shown on the main CheapoDC repository page.
  • The CheapoDC firmware will support from one to six outputs. The first two are assumed to be dew controller outputs but the additional four may be configured as dew controller, PWM or boolean (on/off) outputs.

Parts Required

An AliExpress link is provided for most parts. This is for reference and not necessarily a recommendation to purchase from the link.

Parts List

  • 6 - Dual MOSFET modules, one per output. Cost: US$7.00
  • 1 - LM2596 buck converter module, to reduce 12 volts to 5 volts. Cost: US$1.75
  • 1 - ESP32-C3 SuperMini module. This will often come with the pin headers separate which will need to be soldered to the module. Cost: US$3.25
  • 3 - 12VDC 5.5mm x 2.1mm sockets. One for 12 volt in and two for outputs. Cost: US$2.00
  • 4 - RCA sockets. One for each dew strap output. Cost: US$3.00
  • 1 - SPST Switch. Note that this switch has an integrated status LED which uses the third connector as a ground for the LED. An extra 2N2222 transistor and resistor is required to switch this LED from the ESP32-C3. These are described below with the power board. Cost: US$4.75
  • Some 2.54mm pin male header strips which will be used for the small ESP32-C3 board. Cost: US$1.75
  • 2 - 2.54mm by 8 pin female headers. Used for the small ESP32-C3 board. I used the cheaper 40 pin female headers (link above) and cut them down to size. Cost: US$1.75
  • 2 - 5 amp PPTC resettable fuses. Output loads and ESP32-C3 load will be split across them. Cost: US$2.00
  • 6 - 12VDC Red LEDs. One for each output. Cost: US$2.00
  • Some protoboard or perfboard. To be used for the small ESP32-C3 board and the power board. A single 6cm X 8cm piece should be enough. Cost: US$1.00

Additional Items

  • Several different connectors are used to make the wiring easier. These include dupont connectors, Molex KK-254 connectors and screw terminal connectors. Many of these can be replaced with direct soldering of the wires.
  • Silicone wire. Silicone wire is very flexible and much easier to work with in the small confines of the case used for this project. You will need to pick the gauge of wire used base on your application and expected current handling. My dew straps are 15 to 18 watts or 1.25 to 1.5 amps at 12 volts. Current ratings shown below.
  • An assortment of screws are required to attach the different modules to the 3D printed case.
  • The 3D printed case.

Protoboards

There are two small subassemblies that are with protoboard to make the final assembly easier. A single piece of protoboard may be cut down using a hobby knife to create the two pieces needed for the ESP32-C3 board and the Power board.

ESP32-C3 Board

Power Board

The ESP32-C3 board provides a socket for the ESP32-C3 SuperMini module and the connections to the GPIO pins. This piece of protoboard is 30 mm wide by 25 mm deep. Two 8 pin female 2.54mm pin headers and four 8 pin male 2.54mm pin headers are required. They may be made by cutting down longer 40 pin headers with a hobby knife. The connections are shown in the schematic and are made by soldering wire from pin to pin on the underside of the protoboard. Placing the ESP32-C3 in a socketed mount allows for easy removal of the if required. Next to each socketed pin is a male pin for the corresponding ESP32-C3 pin plus a ground pin. This allows for the use of 2 socket Dupont connectors for the GPIO pins used to control the MOSFET modules and the status LED.

The Power board privdes a place to connect the input power to the two resettable fuses and then to the two MOSFET power lines. It also has the 2N2222 transitor used to switch the LED light in the power switch. This piece of protoboard is 27 mm wide by 32 mm deep. The screw connector blocks, J1 and J2, have 5.04 spaced pins. J3 is a 2 pin male 2.54mm header while J4 is a 2 pin Molex KK-254 male socket.
